1. Interference between 2 struts in series
   The drag of the rear section is increased, the flow separates from the rear of the second strut due to the momentum deficiency of the first strut's wake. 
   The drag of the first section is decreased due to the increase in static pressure between the two struts, pushing the first strut forward.
2. Fairings
   The drag of fairings depends on their slenderness ratio (l/h). Assuming Cf = 0.004 for fairings with higher fineness ratio: CD0 = 0.5Cf(l/h) = 0.002 (l/h). The optimum ratio of such fairings is 10. Min coeff is in the order of CD0=0.03
3. Landing gear 
   Landing gear legs or struts affect the flow pattern past the main body only by a limited part of their length (close to the fuselage/wing surface). The wheels usually do not have a contribution to the interference drag. It is estimated that such a rod attached to a comparatively thick main body (height of strut is 0.5 of the main body diameter d) leads to a 1.7 times increase in drag. Only about 0.32 (0.16d) of the total rod length is responsible for interference drag. This value might be used as a rough rule.
4. Wings (struts) and walls
   Where a wing or strut adjoins a wall (fuselage), and interference drag arises due to the joining if the boundary layers. This drag is independent of the span, its is most suitably based upon the chord area (c^2) or thickness area (t^2).
   The interference drag decreases with moving the wing forward.
5. Tail
   Theoretically, the V-tail leads to the lowest interference drag, since it only has 4 adjoining corners (at the fuselage).
   The horizontal stabilizers have high induced drag since they have to generate a vertical aerodynamic force (for pitch/stability control) and the downwash from the wing increases the effective angle of attack demand. The lift generation goes up, and so does the induced drag.
