**On a conventional aircraft**, the 3 primary control axes are physically separated:

·       Elevator for pitch (tail)

·        Ailerons for roll (wings)

·        Ridder for yaw (tail)

For GLA, this separation is enormously convenient. A conventional GLA architecture uses pitch rate and normal acceleration as feedback signals, and drives the elevator through a low-pass filter for pitch-axis load relief, while ailerons and spoilers handle the wing bending moment — the two loops can be designed and decoupled independently. In practice, the elevator serves as the primary actuator for attitude/pitch control while gust load alleviation is handled by the flaps — and to prevent conflicts between these two loops, their objectives are deliberately decoupled at the architecture level.

**On a flying wing**, that physical separation is gone. Elevons combine elevator and aileron functions: moved symmetrically they produce pitch, moved differentially they produce roll, and both functions can be applied simultaneously. There is no separate tail to absorb pitch-axis gust loads independently of the wing. Every surface you use for GLA is _also_ your primary flight control surface for pitch and roll. Thus, the GLA cannot be designed as a Singe-Input Single-Output (SISO) system. This forces GLA to be a full MIMO problem: a multi-input multi-output adaptive feed-forward control scheme is required to adapt to the layouts of multiple control surfaces and multi-sensors, where the error signals used for updating the adaptive controllers are the outputs of acceleration sensors across the wing.

**On a conventional aircraft**, symmetrically deflecting wing flaps or ailerons for bending moment relief produces almost no net pitching moment — the tail counters any residual with its own separate actuator.

**On a flying wing**, symmetrically deflecting elevons to reduce bending loads _directly_ produces a nose-up or nose-down pitching moment with no independent tail to compensate. Your GLA command and your pitch trim command are competing for the same surfaces at the same time, within the same deflection budget.

Because of the coupling problem, flying-wing GLA research universally adopts multiple spanwise elevon panels rather than a single pair. Among all control surfaces, the outermost surface has the largest deflection amplitude because it has the highest effectiveness in suppressing bending vibration response - but the inner panels are needed to manage the pitch moment generated by those outer deflections. On a conventional aircraft you can get significant GLA benefit from a single pair of outboard ailerons moving symmetrically. You cannot do this on a flying wing without destabilising pitch.

**On a conventional aircraft**, the rudder handles yaw entirely separately. On a flying wing, yaw is produced by differential drag — typically split elevons or spoilerons. This means that any asymmetric gust (one-sided turbulence, lateral gusts) requires the GLA system to negotiate with the yaw control function on the same surfaces simultaneously. There is no equivalent complication on a conventional aircraft.

If the test bed has **one pair of elevons** (minimal flying wing), ==conventional GLA cannot be tested on it at all — there is simply no way to decouple pitch from bending relief.==

If your test bed has **multiple independent spanwise panels** (say, 3–4 per side), ==a reconfigurable control allocator can emulate the conventional architecture with reasonable fidelity: inner panels fixed for pitch, outer panels commanded by GLA.== This allows you to test both architectures with different software configurations on the same airframe.
